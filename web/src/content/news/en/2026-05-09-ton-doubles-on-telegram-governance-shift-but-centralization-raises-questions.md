Toncoin more than doubled in value over a week in early May, climbing from $1.32 to an intraday peak of $2.90, after Pavel Durov announced that Telegram would assume control of TON's governance and become its largest validator. The blockchain's market capitalization pushed toward $7.8 billion on the news.

Durov's statement, paired with updates to ton.org naming Telegram subsidiary MTONGA as the domain controller, left little ambiguity: TON had become Telegram's chain in practice. The market read it as a signal that the app's 1 billion-plus users would form the economic backbone of the network.

The governance announcement capped a series of commercial arrangements formalized in January. Telegram had already locked TON as the exclusive blockchain infrastructure for Mini Apps, made TON Connect the mandatory wallet standard, and restricted Telegram Stars, Premium subscriptions, and advertising payments to Toncoin. Those agreements gave TON structural access to every financial transaction flowing through Telegram's platform.

What followed was rapid product rollout. TON Pay launched in February, institutional stablecoin access arrived in April, and sub-second transaction finality went live the same month—cutting confirmation times from roughly ten seconds to one second. The infrastructure assembled suggests an invisible payments layer embedded inside the messaging app itself.

On-chain metrics reflected the rally's energy. Decentralized exchange volume reached $152.9 million in the week ending May 7, up 1,054% week-over-week. Yet scale comparisons reveal how far TON remains from the networks it would need to displace. Solana holds $15.4 billion in stablecoins versus TON's $752.5 million. TRON, built on stablecoin transfer volume, manages $89.6 billion in assets. TON's closest peer is Sui, with $567.2 million in stablecoins and a $4 billion market cap.

The valuation gap reflects a structural bet: that Telegram can convert its user base into a functioning payment network at scale. Whether that wager holds depends on whether Mini Apps and embedded payments generate authentic adoption.

The uncomfortable centerpiece of the bull case is precisely what drew criticism: Telegram's assumption of governance contradicts the decentralization premise that underpins blockchain's core appeal. A single company now controls both distribution and protocol direction.
