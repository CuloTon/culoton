Pavel Durov has officially repositioned Telegram as the primary force behind The Open Network (TON), moving beyond the independent TON Foundation model that governed the chain since its 2020 launch. The messenger announced it will operate TON's largest validator node, backed by a 2.2 million TON stake—roughly $18 billion at current prices. Markets responded immediately, with Toncoin posting gains exceeding 100% over the rally period.

Durov framed the shift as the third phase of his "Make TON Great Again" roadmap, explicitly tying the blockchain's future to Telegram's trillion-dollar user base. Alexander Tobol, CTO of Wallet in Telegram, told journalists the move represents a fundamental commitment to embedding blockchain infrastructure into the app's core stack. Transfers, payments, and mini-applications would all settle natively on TON, creating what Tobol described as a path to becoming "the leading blockchain by number of active wallets."

Recent technical upgrades strengthen the infrastructure case. The Catchain 2.0 release slashed block finality from ten seconds to one, reduced block times from 2.5 seconds to 400 milliseconds, and cut transaction fees roughly sixfold to $0.0005. The network now claims throughput exceeding 100,000 transactions per second—benchmarks that align with Telegram's stated goal of supporting frequent, low-value commerce.

Denis Vasin, founder of Storm Trade, noted that TON's architecture and Telegram's distribution now match the technological promise investors expected when the project first emerged in 2018. The combination of native user distribution and retail-grade cost structure addresses a gap most Layer-1 blockchains struggle to fill.

Investors face execution risk. TON's price has already repriced substantially, leaving little room for delays in rolling out bot integrations or mini-app transactions. Current staking yields hover near 15% annually, supporting near-term liquidity, but long-term value depends on Telegram converting distribution into sustainable on-chain activity. Holders will monitor whether the validator commitment translates into consistent product development and institutional acceptance.
