A developer building on TON has publicly detailed their experience with the App Center submission process—specifically, what went wrong the first time and how they fixed it.

The timeline tells the story. Twenty days separated their initial rejection from acceptance. That gap was filled with technical audits, SDK integration work, and a complete redesign of key interface elements. The developer's account suggests the review process is not perfunctory.

The first stumble was straightforward but instructive. The team had overlooked a mandatory SDK requirement before submission. The oversight itself is unremarkable; what matters is what it cost them. It forced a return to the technical documentation—a step that, in hindsight, should have been non-negotiable before any submission at all.

Beyond the SDK issue lay a second set of problems: UX and interface design. The reviewers flagged these explicitly enough that the team deemed a full redesign necessary. This suggests the App Center maintains standards beyond mere technical compliance. The platform is curating, not simply cataloging.

For developers now weighing a submission, several takeaways emerge from this experience. Start with the documentation. Read it completely. Implement against the stated requirements first; optimize later. Assume the review process will catch oversights you missed, and plan revision cycles accordingly. A rejection is data, not a verdict.

The twenty-day cycle also illustrates something less obvious: the platform's apparent willingness to work with developers who take feedback seriously. A rejection that leads to improvement and resubmission is not the end of a relationship; it is the beginning of one. The App Center, by this account, is selective but not dismissive.

What emerges is a fairly ordinary story about building software under platform rules. The only thing noteworthy is that it has been told at all—a small but useful contribution to the collective knowledge of what it takes to ship on TON.
