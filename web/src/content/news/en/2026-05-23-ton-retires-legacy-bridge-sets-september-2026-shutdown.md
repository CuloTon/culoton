The Open Network has announced a permanent shutdown of its Toncoin and Token Bridge infrastructure, effective September 1, 2026. The decision concludes a phased deprecation that began in April 2025, when TON disabled outbound Toncoin bridging to Ethereum and BNB Smart Chain, while preserving inbound transfers and withdrawal claims pending a final sunset date.

Users holding Wrapped Toncoin in Ethereum or BNB Smart Chain wallets must return those assets to the TON network before the deadline. Simultaneously, holders of j-tokens on TON—including jUSDT, jUSDC, jDAI, and jWBTC—must bridge those assets back to their origin chains. TON has waived all percentage-based transfer fees through the shutdown period. Bridge oracles will begin withdrawing their staked TON in June 2026 but will continue processing transfers until the final operational cutoff.

Launched in August 2021 for Ethereum and October 2021 for BNB Smart Chain, the bridge served a critical function when Toncoin lacked centralized exchange listings and native on-chain infrastructure. By the time TON announced deprecation last year, the bridge had processed 31,893 transfers, moved over 101 million TON tokens, and maintained a clean security record. At peak usage, Wrapped Toncoin accumulated 35,694 holders on Ethereum and 113,495 on BNB Smart Chain.

The retirement reflects TON's ecosystem maturation. Native USDt stablecoin now operates directly on TON, eliminating the need for wrapped equivalents. A thriving decentralized exchange ecosystem has emerged, and TON has integrated modern cross-chain protocols—LayerZero, Stargate, Symbiosis, and Rhino.fi—that enable direct asset transfers without wrapped-token intermediation. Stargate, built on LayerZero's omnichain messaging, supports transfers across 80+ chains with unified liquidity and near-instant settlement.

TON frames the transition as a security upgrade, removing legacy infrastructure in favour of scalable, modern tools. The bridge's shutdown reflects a broader industry shift toward unified liquidity protocols, though LayerZero itself faced scrutiny following the April 2024 Kelp DAO exploit, prompting some protocols to explore Chainlink alternatives.
