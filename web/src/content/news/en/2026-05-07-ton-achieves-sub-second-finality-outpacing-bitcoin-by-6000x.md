Pavel Durov released performance data this week placing TON's transaction finality at 0.6 seconds, the fastest among Layer 1 blockchains. Bitcoin, by contrast, requires roughly 60 minutes to achieve settlement—a differential of approximately 6,000x.

The benchmark emerged weeks after TON's Catchain 2.0 upgrade, which reduced block times to approximately 400 milliseconds and pushed finality below the one-second threshold on April 10. The comparison positions TON ahead of other high-speed networks: Avalanche, BNB Smart Chain, and Sui all finalize transactions within two seconds, while Solana reaches 13 seconds and Ethereum 13 minutes. Cardano, despite marketing its peer-reviewed proof-of-stake architecture, trails the field at 24 hours.

Bitcoin's extended settlement window reflects its security model—six confirmations paired with 10-minute block intervals provide immutability but sacrifice speed. The gap effectively eliminates Bitcoin as a real-time settlement layer, a concession that underscores the fundamental trade-off between decentralization and transaction speed across blockchains.

Durov also disclosed that Telegram now operates as TON's largest validator, staking approximately 2.2 million tokens valued near $2.9 million. He characterized the position as a mechanism to permit major participants without inducing network centralization. The validator landscape has tightened: competition for roughly 20% staking yield has locked a significant share of TON's supply, reducing circulating float.

The concentration does present governance concerns. Telegram's stake could eventually represent a quarter of total validator power, a figure that raises questions about decision-making authority despite improvements in transaction throughput. TON's price moved sharply higher following the validator disclosure, signaling market perception of Telegram's deepened commitment to the chain.

Whether sustained adoption will materialize remains the open question. Technical speed gains mean little if application-layer activity does not scale proportionally. The network's appeal rests not on benchmark headlines but on whether developers and users find genuine use cases within the TON ecosystem.
