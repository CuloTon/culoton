Belarus has moved to formalize cryptocurrency operations within its banking sector by authorizing cryptobanks to trade a curated list of 26 digital assets. The approved roster includes established names—Bitcoin, Tether, and Toncoin—alongside a broader selection of altcoins, signaling regulatory recognition of the sector's maturity.

The decision reflects a calculated approach to digital asset regulation. Rather than imposing outright bans, Belarusian authorities have opted to create a controlled framework that permits licensed financial institutions to operate in cryptocurrency markets. This strategy mirrors moves by other jurisdictions seeking to capture economic activity while maintaining oversight.

Toncoin's inclusion is notable. The TON blockchain, originally backed by Telegram before its divestment, has grown into an independent ecosystem. Its presence on Belarus's approved list underscores the token's acceptance as a legitimate asset class, even in traditionally skeptical regulatory environments.

The 26-coin whitelist approach offers institutions clarity on permissible trading pairs while limiting exposure to lower-liquidity or higher-risk tokens. Bitcoin and Tether—the de facto reserve currencies of crypto markets—anchor the selection, with the remainder drawn from mid-cap projects with established track records.

For Belarus, the move carries domestic economic weight. The country has faced international sanctions and currency pressures; cryptocurrency operations offer both revenue opportunities through licensing fees and potential financial system flexibility. Cryptobanks operating under license can now offer trading services without legal ambiguity.

The framework's long-term viability depends on how enforcement evolves and whether the approved coin list expands or contracts based on market conditions. Early signals suggest Belarus intends to maintain the structure as a permanent regulatory pillar rather than a temporary experiment.
